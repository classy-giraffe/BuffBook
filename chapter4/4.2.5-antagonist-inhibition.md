### 4.2.5: Minimizes Antagonist Inhibition

Whenever an agonist muscle contracts, the nervous system automatically inhibits its antagonist—a safety mechanism called **reciprocal inhibition**. For example, activating the quadriceps relaxes the hamstrings, and contracting the biceps inhibits the triceps. In many compound lifts, antagonists are inevitably co-activated for joint stability (e.g., hamstrings co-contract during squats to protect the knee). This co-contraction reduces the net external force experienced by the target muscle, because the antagonist is actively fighting the movement.

For hypertrophy, you want to minimize unnecessary antagonist co-contraction so that the prime mover experiences the highest possible tension. This is one reason machines, which provide external stability, often outperform free weights for isolation: when the stabilising burden is removed, the nervous system reduces antagonist co-contraction, allowing more of the total force production to come from the intended muscle [3]. Similarly, exercises that preposition the antagonist into a shortened or slackened state can further reduce its activation. The overhead triceps extension, for instance, stretches the long head of the triceps while putting the biceps into a shortened position, reducing biceps co-contraction relative to a pushdown.

**Practical Takeaway:** Choose exercises and equipment that allow the target muscle to be the primary force producer without requiring high levels of simultaneous antagonist engagement.
 When using free weights for compounds, accept that some co-contraction is unavoidable, but understand that it shifts some of the training stimulus away from the prime mover and toward the stabilizers. For isolation work, let machines and cables do the stabilising so the muscle you care about does the contracting.
