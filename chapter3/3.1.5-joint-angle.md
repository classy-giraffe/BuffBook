### 3.1.5: Joint Angle, Exercise Selection, and the Resistance Profile

The influence of muscle length on regional hypertrophy is not merely theoretical. Maeo et al. (2021) compared seated leg curls (where the biarticular hamstrings are trained at a long muscle length, with the hip flexed) to prone leg curls (where the hamstrings are trained at a shorter length, with the hip extended). After 12 weeks, the seated leg curl produced significantly greater growth of the whole hamstrings (+14% vs +9%) and each biarticular head, with the largest advantage seen in the distal region [10]. The monoarticular hamstring head (biceps femoris short head), which is less affected by hip angle, showed no difference between conditions—confirming that the effect was driven specifically by muscle length.

Similarly, Pedrosa et al. (2022) found that partial range-of-motion biceps curls performed at long muscle lengths (the lengthened portion of the curl) produced at least as much elbow flexor hypertrophy as full-ROM training, and substantially more than partials performed at short muscle lengths [11]. Other research groups have reported that the incline biceps curl (which places the long head of the biceps under a greater stretch) preferentially grows the distal region of the biceps compared to the preacher curl, which loads the muscle at a shorter length [21].

The pattern is remarkably consistent: exercises that challenge a muscle in its lengthened position produce robust whole-muscle hypertrophy and tend to bias growth toward the distal regions. However, the Varovic et al. (2025) systematic review and meta-analysis urges some interpretive caution. When pooling all available studies, the differences in regional hypertrophy between exercises are often small and often fall within regions of practical equivalence, particularly when the average difference in muscle length between conditions was modest (approximately 22%) [15]. In other words, training at longer muscle lengths likely provides an advantage, but the magnitude of that advantage may be smaller than some enthusiasts claim, and it almost certainly depends on how large a length difference the exercise selection actually creates.

The practical expression of this principle involves the **resistance profile** of the exercise. Exercises can have:

- **Ascending profiles:** resistance increases as the joint extends (e.g., squat, leg press). Here, the muscle is challenged most at short muscle lengths.
- **Descending profiles:** resistance is greatest at long muscle lengths and decreases as the joint extends (e.g., many cam-based machines specifically designed to load the stretched position).
- **Bell-shaped profiles:** resistance peaks in the mid-range (e.g., free-weight bicep curls, where the external moment arm is longest at 90° of elbow flexion).

**The practical implication—to be expanded in Chapter 4—is that exercises emphasizing the lengthened position deserve a prominent place in a hypertrophy program, but exclusive reliance on them is unnecessary.**
