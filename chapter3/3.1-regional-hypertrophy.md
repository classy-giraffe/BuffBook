## 3.1 Regional Hypertrophy: How to influence *where* your muscles grow

Muscles do not grow uniformly as a single, shapeless block; instead, they exhibit *regional hypertrophy*, meaning growth can be biased toward specific areas within the muscle based on how it is trained. To understand this, we first need to define anatomical directions:

- **Proximal** refers to the region of a muscle closest to the center of the body or the root of the limb.
- **Distal** refers to the region furthest from the center of the body.

The exact region that experiences the most growth is dictated by three primary physiological drivers:

- **Neuromechanical matching**: the regions of a muscle with the best leverage for a given movement are preferentially activated, and therefore grow the most.
- **Two-joint muscle principle**: the region that grows most depends on which joint the muscle is acting upon.
- **Regional variations in passive mechanical tension**: stretch-mediated hypertrophy (*sarcomerogenesis*) can bias growth toward the distal region when muscles are trained at long muscle lengths or in stretched positions, such as during eccentric-only training or full range-of-motion exercises.

However, distal growth is not always an indicator of sarcomerogenesis, which brings us to the **two-joint muscle principle**. Two-joint muscles, like the rectus femoris, hamstrings, and biceps brachii, display a distinctive pattern of regional activation and subsequent hypertrophy depending on which joint they are acting upon. They preferentially activate and grow their **proximal** region when working at the proximal joint, and their **distal** region when working at the distal joint.

For example:

- The rectus femoris grows more proximally when trained with hip flexion exercises, but grows more distally when trained with knee extension exercises.
- The biceps brachii shows greater proximal growth when acting as a shoulder flexor, and greater distal growth when acting as an elbow flexor.

Therefore, the distal hypertrophy often seen in two-joint muscles during single-joint exercises, like knee extensions or curls, is often driven simply by regional leverage and activation rather than stretch-mediated sarcomerogenesis.

Ultimately, by strategically manipulating **exercise selection**, **joint angles**, and **range of motion**, you can target specific regions of a muscle to align with your aesthetic or functional goals.
