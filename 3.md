# Chapter 3: The Specificity of Hypertrophy

In Chapters 1 and I2, we established that the non-negotiable trigger for muscle growth is the combination of high-threshold motor unit recruitment and high fiber-level mechanical tension. Any training method that checks both boxes will stimulate hypertrophy, regardless of the load on the bar, the number of reps, or the subjective sensation of burn and pump. But once those fundamental conditions are met, two more nuanced questions arise.

First, **is hypertrophy task-specific?** Within a given muscle, can we target *specific regions*? The evidence says yes: the joint angle, muscle length, and resistance profile of an exercise can bias growth toward one end of a muscle or another. Second, **is hypertrophy fiber-type specific?** Do Type I and Type II fibers demand fundamentally different training approaches? The answer here is more subtle: while fiber types exhibit different absolute growth capacities and may show proportional shifts under extreme conditions, the same core mechanisms—high MUR and high tension—stimulate both. Understanding these two layers of specificity is what transforms a generic training program into one that sculpts a physique intelligently. The practical *application* of these principles—which exercises to pick and how to program them—will then be the subject of Chapters 4 and 5.

## 3.1 Regional Hypertrophy: Why the Shape of the Load Matters

A muscle does not grow as a uniform block. The quadriceps, hamstrings, triceps, and pectorals all display regional differences in hypertrophy depending on how they are trained. Understanding why this happens is one of the most practically valuable skills in exercise selection. Two major theoretical frameworks have emerged to explain regional hypertrophy: **Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy (SMH)** and **Neuromechanical Matching (NMM)**. While they are sometimes presented as opposing views, they may in fact describe complementary mechanisms that operate through different pathways and potentially produce different structural outcomes. This section presents both theories and examines where they agree, where they diverge, and what the current evidence actually supports.

### 3.1.1: Competing Theories for Regional Hypertrophy: SMH vs. NMM

The tension-based model of hypertrophy established in Chapters 1 and 2 leaves an important question unanswered: given that a muscle is being subjected to high mechanical tension, *where along its length* will the growth occur? Two distinct hypotheses attempt to answer this.

| Dimension | Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy (SMH) | Neuromechanical Matching (NMM) |
|---|---|---|
| **Core premise** | Regional growth is driven by the magnitude of passive mechanical tension a region experiences when the muscle is elongated under load. Regions placed under greater stretch receive a larger stimulus [1]. | Regional growth is driven by which motor units are *recruited*. The nervous system activates muscle regions in proportion to their leverage—their internal moment arm—at a given joint angle [2,3]. |
| **Primary mechanism** | Passive tension transmitted through titin and the Z-disc, activating mechanosensitive signaling pathways (including mTORC1) independently of neural activation [4,5]. | Differential motor unit recruitment based on regional leverage. Fibers that are not activated cannot grow, regardless of how much passive stretch the region experiences [6]. |
| **Type of growth predicted** | Increases in fascicle length via sarcomerogenesis in series—the addition of new sarcomeres end-to-end [1,7]. This biases growth toward *longitudinal* hypertrophy rather than cross-sectional area. | Standard myofibrillar hypertrophy (increases in cross-sectional area and diameter) in whichever region has the greatest leverage for the exercise [3,8]. |
| **Role of activation** | Passive tension can stimulate some growth even without muscle activation, as demonstrated in denervated muscle stretching studies in animals [5]. However, combining passive tension with activation (as in loaded eccentrics) amplifies the effect [1]. | Activation is **mandatory**. A muscle fiber must be recruited via its motor neuron to experience active tension and trigger the full mechanotransduction cascade [6]. |
| **Primary evidence base** | Animal stretching studies (e.g., denervated muscle growth), lengthened partial ROM human trials (Maeo et al. 2021; Pedrosa et al. 2022), static stretching hypertrophy studies [9,10,11]. | Animal and human studies on differential motor unit activation based on moment arm (established in respiratory muscles), regional EMG patterns correlating with internal moment arm length, and computational modeling [2,12,13]. |
| **Key advocate(s)** | Broad consensus in the sports science community, with varied definitions. Both Beardsley (emphasizing pure passive tension) and researchers like Schoenfeld, Henselmans, and Nuckols (emphasizing lengthened-position dynamic training) have contributed to the concept [14]. | Primarily developed and championed by Chris Beardsley as a framework for exercise selection [3,8]. |
| **Respiratory muscle precedent** | Not applicable in the same way. | Well-established: the diaphragm and intercostal muscles display clear NMM, with neural drive distributed to regions with the most favorable mechanical advantage during different breathing tasks [12,13]. |

### 3.1.2: Where the Theories Agree

Despite their differences, SMH and NMM are not entirely contradictory. Two critical points of agreement exist:

1. **The lengthened position is special.** Both theories converge on the practical recommendation that exercises which challenge the muscle in a stretched position are highly effective for hypertrophy. SMH explains this through passive tension; NMM explains it through favorable leverage at long muscle lengths for certain regions. Either way, the practical advice is the same.

2. **Both mechanisms can coexist.** A muscle fiber experiencing a loaded stretch during a resistance exercise is simultaneously being activated (via NMM-governed recruitment) and experiencing passive tension (via titin strain). The two mechanisms likely operate in parallel, and the relative contribution of each may depend on the specific exercise, load, range of motion, and training history of the lifter [6].

### 3.1.3: The Scientific Status of Both Models

#### 3.1.3.1: Neuromechanical Matching: Plausible but Unproven in Limb Muscles

As described in Section 3.1.1, NMM is well-established in the respiratory muscles. The diaphragm, for instance, exhibits a clear neuromechanical matching pattern whereby neural drive is distributed to regions with the most favorable mechanical advantage during different breathing tasks [12,13]. In limb skeletal muscles, however, direct evidence that moment arm differences *cause* the predicted regional hypertrophy patterns, as opposed to simply correlating with them, is still accumulating. Factors such as passive tension gradients along the muscle, regional differences in fiber type composition, and inter-individual variation in internal moment arms all complicate the picture. As Varovic et al. (2025) noted in their systematic review and meta-analysis, the measured differences in regional hypertrophy between exercises are often small, and the field has not yet isolated leverage as the sole explanatory variable [15].

NMM is therefore best treated as the leading theoretical framework for exercise selection—a hypothesis with substantial supporting evidence and strong mechanistic plausibility, but one that should be applied with an experimental mindset rather than dogmatic certainty. The concrete process of evaluating a machine's resistance profile and matching it to a target muscle's leverage will be detailed in Chapter 4.

#### 3.1.3.2: Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy: Real Effect, Ongoing Debate About Mechanism

SMH has accumulated an impressive body of supporting evidence. Animal studies dating back to the 1970s have demonstrated that purely passive muscle stretching can stimulate robust muscle growth [5,16]. Human studies have shown that lengthened partial ROM training produces at least equivalent—and often superior—hypertrophy compared to full ROM or shortened-position training [10,11]. Static stretching protocols, when performed with sufficient intensity and frequency, can produce measurable increases in muscle thickness and fascicle length [17].

However, significant debate remains about the *nature* of this growth. The original SMH model, as articulated by Beardsley, proposes that passive tension stimulates the addition of sarcomeres in series (longitudinal hypertrophy), which may not translate into increases in muscle cross-sectional area or "thickness" in the traditional sense [1,7]. If true, this would mean SMH contributes more to muscle *lengthening* than to the kind of transverse growth that bodybuilders typically pursue. However, multiple human studies have observed significant increases in muscle thickness and CSA from training that emphasizes the lengthened position [9,10], and researchers like Henselmans have argued that SMH likely involves conventional myofibrillar hypertrophy in addition to any longitudinal changes [14].

The debate is further complicated by terminology. As Beardsley himself has acknowledged, the term "stretch-mediated hypertrophy" has been used in multiple, conflicting ways: by some to refer exclusively to passive-tension-driven sarcomerogenesis, by others to describe any hypertrophy that results from training at longer muscle lengths, regardless of the mechanism [14]. For the practical lifter, the semantic debate matters less than the consistent finding: exercises that place high tension on a muscle in the lengthened position tend to stimulate robust hypertrophy, and including such exercises in a program is a sensible strategy.

### 3.1.4: The Length-Tension Landscape: The Shared Foundation

Regardless of which theoretical model one prefers, both NMM and SMH rest on the same underlying physiology: the **length-tension curve** of skeletal muscle. Every sarcomere, the basic contractile unit of muscle, has an optimal length at which actin and myosin filaments overlap enough to generate maximum active force (the plateau of the length-tension curve). When a muscle fiber is stretched beyond this optimum, active tension declines because there are fewer cross-bridge binding sites available, but *passive tension* rises sharply. This passive tension is generated by the giant protein titin, which acts like a molecular spring that resists stretch and literally pulls the sarcomere apart from within [4,18]. The Z-disc, the structural anchor at both ends of the sarcomere, acts as a mechanosensor that detects this titin-mediated strain and transmits it into the same growth-signaling cascades (including mTORC1) we described in Chapter 1 [19,20].

This dual source of tension—active from cross-bridge cycling plus passive from titin strain—means that when a muscle fiber contracts at a long length, it experiences greater total deformation than when it contracts at a short length. And since mechanotransduction responds to total deformation, the stretched position provides a uniquely potent stimulus. Critically, passive tension is not evenly distributed across all regions of most muscles. Proximal and distal fibers often operate on different parts of the length-tension curve during a given exercise because of differences in sarcomere number, pennation angle, and moment arm, which means a region that experiences more passive stretch will be exposed to a greater total growth stimulus than one that does not.

### 3.1.5: Joint Angle, Exercise Selection, and the Resistance Profile

The influence of muscle length on regional hypertrophy is not merely theoretical. Maeo et al. (2021) compared seated leg curls (where the biarticular hamstrings are trained at a long muscle length, with the hip flexed) to prone leg curls (where the hamstrings are trained at a shorter length, with the hip extended). After 12 weeks, the seated leg curl produced significantly greater growth of the whole hamstrings (+14% vs +9%) and each biarticular head, with the largest advantage seen in the distal region [10]. The monoarticular hamstring head (biceps femoris short head), which is less affected by hip angle, showed no difference between conditions—confirming that the effect was driven specifically by muscle length.

Similarly, Pedrosa et al. (2022) found that partial range-of-motion biceps curls performed at long muscle lengths (the lengthened portion of the curl) produced at least as much elbow flexor hypertrophy as full-ROM training, and substantially more than partials performed at short muscle lengths [11]. Other research groups have reported that the incline biceps curl (which places the long head of the biceps under a greater stretch) preferentially grows the distal region of the biceps compared to the preacher curl, which loads the muscle at a shorter length [21].

The pattern is remarkably consistent: exercises that challenge a muscle in its lengthened position produce robust whole-muscle hypertrophy and tend to bias growth toward the distal regions. However, the Varovic et al. (2025) systematic review and meta-analysis urges some interpretive caution. When pooling all available studies, the differences in regional hypertrophy between longer and shorter mean muscle lengths were small and often fell within regions of practical equivalence, particularly when the average difference in muscle length between conditions was modest (approximately 22%) [15]. In other words, training at longer muscle lengths likely provides an advantage, but the magnitude of that advantage may be smaller than some enthusiasts claim, and it almost certainly depends on how large a length difference the exercise selection actually creates.

The practical expression of this principle involves the **resistance profile** of the exercise. Exercises can have:

- **Ascending profiles:** resistance increases as the joint extends (e.g., squat, leg press). Here, the muscle is challenged most at short muscle lengths.
- **Descending profiles:** resistance is greatest at long muscle lengths and decreases as the joint extends (e.g., many cam-based machines specifically designed to load the stretched position).
- **Bell-shaped profiles:** resistance peaks in the mid-range (e.g., free-weight bicep curls, where the external moment arm is longest at 90° of elbow flexion).

**The practical implication—to be expanded in Chapter 4—is that exercises emphasizing the lengthened position deserve a prominent place in a hypertrophy program, but exclusive reliance on them is unnecessary.**

## 3.2 Contraction Type: Eccentrics, Concentrics, and the Stretch Effect

### 3.2.1: Eccentric vs. Concentric Hypertrophy

During an eccentric (lengthening) contraction, a muscle fiber can produce 20–50% more force than during a concentric contraction at the same speed, because titin binds to actin during stretch, adding passive resistance to active cross-bridge force [4,18]. In principle, if high-threshold motor units are recruited and the bar speed is slow, the eccentrically contracting fiber experiences greater mechanical tension than during the subsequent concentric phase, potentially providing a stronger growth stimulus per rep. This is relevant to both the SMH and NMM frameworks: SMH because eccentric contractions maximize passive tension, and NMM because the motor units must still be recruited for this tension to translate into the full mechanotransduction cascade.

The practical application must navigate a trade-off. Accentuated eccentric training—where the lifter deliberately slows the lowering phase or uses supramaximal eccentric loads—undoubtedly increases fiber-level tension and can promote hypertrophy. However, it also substantially increases muscle damage. As we established in Section 2.2c (Myth #3), excessive damage diverts cellular resources toward repair and can directly impair mTORC1 signaling via inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α [22]. The eccentric phase is, therefore, a double-edged sword: a moderate emphasis provides additional mechanical stimulus, but a severe emphasis creates a damage burden that undercuts the very growth it is meant to stimulate.

**The principle is clear:** some controlled eccentric work is likely beneficial for maximizing tension-mediated signals, but the specific parameters (tempo, load, volume allocation) that balance this benefit against damage are programming questions that belong to Chapter 5. Chapter 2 has already made the case that damage is not a goal, and Chapter 5 will provide concrete guidelines for applying this principle without falling into the recovery trap.

### 3.2.2: The Lengthened Emphasis: Principle Without Prescription

Given the converging evidence that training at long muscle lengths is beneficial, the conceptual takeaway is straightforward: exercises that load the target muscle in a stretched position provide a stimulus component that shorter-length exercises do not, whether the primary mechanism is passive tension (SMH), favorable leverage and full motor unit recruitment (NMM), or—most likely—a combination of both.

How exactly to incorporate lengthened training—exercise selection, partial range of motion as a supplement or replacement, load modulation—is not a subject for this chapter. Those choices are the domain of exercise selection (Chapter 4) and programming volume, intensity, and frequency (Chapter 5). The role of this section is simply to establish that **the capacity to bias growth by manipulating muscle length and contraction type is a real, evidence-based layer of hypertrophy specificity**, not a myth to be dismissed.

## 3.3 But What About Fiber Types? (A Clarification, Not a Program)

### 3.3.1: Do Type I and Type II Fibers Grow Differently?

Yes, Type II (fast-twitch) fibers possess greater *absolute* growth potential than Type I (slow-twitch) fibers. They are larger at baseline, contain more ribosomes, and upregulate protein synthesis more robustly after mechanical loading. However, both fiber types hypertrophy meaningfully when they are recruited and subjected to high mechanical tension. In this sense, hypertrophy is **fiber-type specific** in its *magnitude*, but the *mechanism* that triggers it is the same: high MUR and high fiber-level tension.

A systematic review and meta-analysis by Grgic and Schoenfeld (2018) examined whether high-load (≥60% 1RM) and low-load (<60% 1RM) resistance training produce fiber-type-specific hypertrophy. The analysis found that both Type I and Type II fibers grew with both loading schemes, and that low-load RT did *not* selectively grow Type I fibers at the expense of Type II fibers when sets were taken close to or to failure [23]. In other words, when effort is high and bar speed is slow on the final reps, both fiber types receive the necessary stimulus, regardless of the external load.

There is, however, some evidence that very high-volume, low-load training to failure may produce a proportionally greater Type I fiber response in certain muscles with a high baseline Type I composition. This is not because light loads are a "Type I-specific signal," but likely because the enormous cumulative time under tension under fatigued conditions means the recruited Type I fibers are stimulated for a longer total duration. This limited, context-dependent fiber-type specificity does not break the central model; it merely adds a minor programming consideration for advanced athletes seeking to exhaust every avenue, a topic we will revisit in Chapter 5.

### 3.3.2: Why "Train Slow for Slow-Twitch" Is a Distraction

The common belief that Type I fibers require deliberately slow, controlled repetitions stems from a misunderstanding of the force-velocity curve and motor unit recruitment. As established in Chapter 2, motor unit recruitment is governed by the level of effort, not the intentional speed of movement. When you train with a light load (e.g., 30% 1RM) and take the set to failure, the early reps feel easy, recruit only low-threshold motor units, and are therefore non-stimulating. But as fatigue accumulates, the central motor command escalates, eventually recruiting high-threshold motor units. By the time these Type II fibers are recruited, the bar has naturally slowed down because the muscle is fatigued, so the force-velocity condition for high fiber-level tension is satisfied automatically.

If you deliberately artificially slow down the rep from the very first repetition of a light-load set (e.g., a 5-second concentric), you are not increasing the recruitment of high-threshold motor units. You are simply making the first few non-stimulating reps take longer, increasing the total metabolic cost of the set and creating a sensation of effort that is primarily metabolic, not mechanical. This "super-slow" approach does not add tension where tension is needed and therefore does not meaningfully enhance the growth of either fiber type.

The only scenario in which deliberate slowness provides theoretical benefit is with heavy loads. Heavy loads (≥80% 1RM) recruit high-threshold motor units from the very first rep regardless of bar speed, but lifting explosively with a heavy load still creates low fiber-level tension because of the force-velocity relationship. For this reason, intentionally controlling rep speed when training in low rep ranges (1–5 RM) ensures that the recruited Type II fibers experience sufficient tension to trigger mechanotransduction.

### Key takeaways

- **Hypertrophy is region-specific.** Muscles grow non-uniformly. SMH (passive tension) and NMM (leverage-based recruitment) both explain why the lengthened position is uniquely effective. The practical lesson: prioritize exercises that challenge muscles at long lengths to stimulate growth across the widest portion of the muscle.
- **Lengthened training is a tool, not a dogma.** The effect is real but often modest; the magnitude depends on how much extra length the exercise creates. Apply it with flexibility, not rigidity.
- **Eccentrics amplify tension but also damage.** A controlled eccentric (2–3 seconds) maximizes mechanical stimulus without excessive damage. Extreme eccentric overload is counterproductive.
- **Fiber types don’t require separate programs.** Both Type I and Type II fibers grow when recruited under high mechanical tension. Rep range, load, and tempo are programming trade-offs for recovery and fatigue management—not tools to “target” specific fiber types.
- **The bottom line:** Proximity to failure dictates which motor units are recruited; bar speed determines how much tension those recruited fibers experience. Use heavy or moderate loads and allow the bar to slow naturally as fatigue rises. Deliberately slow reps with light loads do not bypass the need for high-threshold motor unit recruitment and are mostly a waste of training time.
