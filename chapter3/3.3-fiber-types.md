## 3.3 But What About Fiber Types? (A Clarification, Not a Program)

### 3.3.1: Do Type I and Type II Fibers Grow Differently?

Yes, Type II (fast-twitch) fibers possess greater *absolute* growth potential than Type I (slow-twitch) fibers. They are larger at baseline, contain more ribosomes, and upregulate protein synthesis more robustly after mechanical loading. However, both fiber types hypertrophy meaningfully when they are recruited and subjected to high mechanical tension. In this sense, hypertrophy is **fiber-type specific** in its *magnitude*, but the *mechanism* that triggers it is the same: high MUR and high fiber-level tension.

A systematic review and meta-analysis by Grgic and Schoenfeld (2018) examined whether high-load (≥60% 1RM) and low-load (<60% 1RM) resistance training produce fiber-type-specific hypertrophy. The analysis found that both Type I and Type II fibers grew with both loading schemes, and that low-load RT did *not* selectively grow Type I fibers at the expense of Type II fibers when sets were taken close to or to failure [23]. In other words, when effort is high and bar speed is slow on the final reps, both fiber types receive the necessary stimulus, regardless of the external load.

There is, however, some evidence that very high-volume, low-load training to failure may produce a proportionally greater Type I fiber response in certain muscles with a high baseline Type I composition. This is not because light loads are a "Type I-specific signal," but likely because the enormous cumulative time under tension under fatigued conditions means the recruited Type I fibers are stimulated for a longer total duration. This limited, context-dependent fiber-type specificity does not break the central model; it merely adds a minor programming consideration for advanced athletes seeking to exhaust every avenue, a topic we will revisit in Chapter 5.

### 3.3.2: Why \"Train Slow for Slow-Twitch\" Is a Distraction

The common belief that Type I fibers require deliberately slow, controlled repetitions stems from a misunderstanding of the force-velocity curve and motor unit recruitment. As established in Chapter 2, motor unit recruitment is governed by the level of effort, not the intentional speed of movement. When you train with a light load (e.g., 30% 1RM) and take the set to failure, the early reps feel easy, recruit only low-threshold motor units, and are therefore non-stimulating. But as fatigue accumulates, the central motor command escalates, eventually recruiting high-threshold motor units. By the time these Type II fibers are recruited, the bar has naturally slowed down because the muscle is fatigued, so the force-velocity condition for high fiber-level tension is satisfied automatically.

If you deliberately artificially slow down the rep from the very first repetition of a light-load set (e.g., a 5-second concentric), you are not increasing the recruitment of high-threshold motor units. You are simply making the first few non-stimulating reps take longer, increasing the total metabolic cost of the set and creating a sensation of effort that is primarily metabolic, not mechanical. This \"super-slow\" approach does not add tension where tension is needed and therefore does not meaningfully enhance the growth of either fiber type.

The only scenario in which deliberate slowness provides theoretical benefit is with heavy loads. Heavy loads (≥80% 1RM) recruit high-threshold motor units from the very first rep regardless of bar speed, but lifting explosively with a heavy load still creates low fiber-level tension because of the force-velocity relationship. For this reason, intentionally controlling rep speed when training in low rep ranges (1–5 RM) ensures that the recruited Type II fibers experience sufficient tension to trigger mechanotransduction.
