### 3.1.5: Joint Angle, Exercise Selection, and the Resistance Profile

The influence of muscle length on regional hypertrophy is not merely theoretical. It is consistently observed in practical research.

#### The Evidence: Research in Practice
*   **Hamstrings:** Maeo et al. (2021) compared seated leg curls (stretched hip) to prone leg curls (extended hip). The seated curl produced significantly greater growth (**+14% vs +9%**), with the largest advantage seen in the distal (lower) region [10].
*   **Biceps:** Pedrosa et al. (2022) found that "lengthened partials" (performing only the stretched half of a curl) produced more elbow flexor hypertrophy than partials performed in the shortened position [11].
*   **Specific Heads:** Research shows that incline biceps curls (higher stretch) preferentially grow the distal region of the biceps compared to preacher curls, which load the muscle at a shorter length [21].

#### A Note of Caution
As the Varovic et al. (2025) meta-analysis notes, while the advantage of the lengthened position is real, the magnitude can be small [15]. The benefit depends heavily on **how much extra length** the exercise selection actually creates. Training at longer muscle lengths provides a high-probability advantage, but it is not a "magic bullet."

#### Understanding Resistance Profiles
The practical expression of these principles involves the **resistance profile** of an exercise. This describes where the movement is hardest:

*   **Ascending profiles:** Resistance increases as the joint extends (e.g., Squat, Leg Press). The muscle is challenged most at **short** lengths.
*   **Descending profiles:** Resistance is greatest at the start and decreases (e.g., cam-based machines designed to load the **stretched** position).
*   **Bell-shaped profiles:** Resistance peaks in the mid-range (e.g., free-weight Biceps Curls).

**Practical Implication:** Exercises that emphasize the lengthened position deserve a prominent place in a program, but exclusive reliance on them is unnecessary. We will expand on how to assemble these into a "menu" in Chapter 4.
